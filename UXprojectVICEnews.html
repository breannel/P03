Vice Perspectives Chatbot Overview

US Case Study // Fall 2017

For a senior UX design class, I selected, researched, and designed a digital solution and pitch for VICE News. To address a market oversaturated with video content, my team and I designed a chatbot that delivers first-hand, journalistic news through a personable, chatbot interface.

Design Overview

Vice is projected to miss their target revenue as of late 2017, and it’s not just them. Their industry is oversaturated — everyone shifted into long form video content at the same time. How might we leverage what’s always made them different: under reported news and gritty frontline perspectives told by relatable reporters that tell you stories like a friend would, not a corporation?

Framing the Problem

Vox, Buzzfeed, Vice... all of these digital media groups have moved to video content platforms rather than written articles. Problem is, since everyone shifted to video, the market has become oversaturated and Vice is projected to miss their target revenue as of late 2017 as illustrated above.

Using the success of their reporting on Charlottesville as a key example, we asked how might we leverage what’s always made them different: under reported news and gritty frontline perspectives told by relatable reporters that tell you stories like a friend would, not a corporation?

Brand and Tone

My brand analysis of Vice News surfaced 4 themes for our team: Raw, Hard-hitting, Deferential, and Intimate. Vice is unique in that people tend to see the brand as a person, they think their audience is smarter than them and cultivates the image of a knowledgeable friend that tells you like it is. We analyzed the Cindy Gallop chatbot (a screenshot of this chatbot can be seen above) for ideas on tone and feasibility.

User Frictions with News articles

Based on a 5 day SPRINT, our users also noted that "articles can be kind of bland - photos, text, done". In summary, news is often dry and hard to digest and they often can't be bothered to seek it out; they prefer having a conversation about news than just reading dry facts.

First Prototype

Our first prototype was clunky, but it surfaced a major question from our users: "Why a chatbot? Why not just let me talk to the journalist?". It was a valid issue, chatbots are trendy; a buzzword. When people think of a chatbot, they usually think of a personal assistant or a support desk. They also raised issues about onboarding, it wasn't clear that they were talking to a bot, they thought someone was speaking in real-time with them and felt betrayed when it wasn't a real person.

There were also ethical issues surrounding showing explicit content to users when they were unprepared that absolutely needed to be corrected.

Revised Prototype

Instead of returning to the drawing board, we stuck to our guns. Our goal was to create a conversation and tell an engaging story with a defined personality without putting strain on Vice News staff and resources. This conversational way of delivering information is what our persona wants, what Vice is good at, and it’s why we chose this solution.

However, we still needed to address the issues and objections brought up during testing, so I spent days refining copy, ensuring the user was properly onboarded into the process, and also ensuring users had options for the explicit content shown in the experience, which can be seen above.

Value Proposition

Our intervention adds value to Vice in 3 ways: data collection from user choices that can be used to refine the experience, bring in outside viewership as current users share their thoughts on social media and this potentially engages friends who aren’t Vice followers, and further differentiate in an oversaturated market by remixing their raw, first-hand coverage and journalist personalities.

Reflection

I am satisfied with this experience, but I have some caveats to add. People who prefer traditional, non-partisan news simply will not engage with this media, and readers that have different beliefs or perspectives from the journalists may feel antagonized or excluded.

I also know that "fake" news and journalism in general are topics greatly debated at the moment in the US, but I do believe that this intervention fits the persona that Vice portrays themselves as; it's controversial, sparks conversation, and assumes that audiences are smart enough to know the difference between fact and opinion. As such, I have some reservations about our solution as is. I think it's a great starting point, but it needs careful testing and ethical thought put into it before it could ever launch.
